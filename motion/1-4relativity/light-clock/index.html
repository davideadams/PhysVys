SACE Stage 2 Physics — Motion 1.4

Light Clock

A photon bouncing between two parallel mirrors makes the perfect clock — one tick per round trip. Compare the same clock in its rest frame and a lab frame where it moves at velocity \(v\). The lab observer must accept the photon traces a longer diagonal path — and since the speed of light is the same in every frame, the moving clock must tick slower by a factor \(\gamma = 1/\sqrt{1-v^{2}/c^{2}}\).

Mirror frame: You're sitting next to the clock. The photon travels straight up, distance \(L\), then straight back down. One full tick takes the proper time \(T_0 = 2L/c\).
Lab frame: The clock drifts past you at velocity \(v\). The photon still moves at \(c\), but its path is now a diagonal "tent" — one full tick takes the dilated time \(t = \gamma T_0\), longer than \(T_0\).

Deriving time dilation from the light clock

  1. Mirror frame. The photon makes one round trip — up \(L\), back down \(L\) — in proper time \(T_0 = \dfrac{2L}{c}\).
  2. Lab frame. In time \(t\), the clock moves a horizontal distance \(vt\). The photon traces two diagonal segments forming a tent of base \(vt\) and height \(L\) — but the postulate of relativity insists the photon's speed is still \(c\), so each diagonal has length \(\dfrac{ct}{2}\).
  3. Pythagoras on one diagonal: \(\left(\dfrac{ct}{2}\right)^{2} = L^{2} + \left(\dfrac{vt}{2}\right)^{2}\).
  4. Solve for \(t\). Expand the squares and rearrange:
    \(\dfrac{c^{2}t^{2}}{4} = L^{2} + \dfrac{v^{2}t^{2}}{4}\)
    \(\dfrac{c^{2}t^{2}}{4} - \dfrac{v^{2}t^{2}}{4} = L^{2}\)
    \(\dfrac{t^{2}}{4}\!\left(c^{2} - v^{2}\right) = L^{2}\)
    \(t^{2} = \dfrac{4L^{2}}{c^{2} - v^{2}}\)
    \(t = \dfrac{2L}{\sqrt{c^{2} - v^{2}}}\)
    Factor \(c\) out of the surd:
    \(t = \dfrac{2L}{c\sqrt{1 - v^{2}/c^{2}}} = \dfrac{2L/c}{\sqrt{1 - v^{2}/c^{2}}}\)
  5. Recognise \(T_0 = 2L/c\) and \(\gamma = 1/\sqrt{1-v^{2}/c^{2}}\): \(\;\boxed{\,t = \gamma\,T_0\,}\). The moving clock ticks slower.
