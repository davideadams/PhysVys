Step 1

Synchronising the Clocks

Before making any measurements we must agree on synchronised clocks in each frame.

  1. Station clocks: A flash is set off midway between the two station clocks. The two photons travel equal distances and arrive simultaneously. We set both clocks to the same reading when the photons arrive. ✓
  2. Train clocks: We repeat the exact same procedure on the moving train — a flash from the midpoint of the train. Because we cannot tell by any experiment whether we are moving, we must use the same rule. The front clock must be set back relative to a naive guess, so that both photons arrive at the same clock reading. The train clocks are now synchronised in the train's own frame.

As a check: measure the travel time of a photon between adjacent clocks in each direction. The result is the same each way — confirming that the speed of light is constant in every frame.

Step 2

Length Measurements

We place a standard 100 cm metre stick on the train and measure its length from the station, and vice versa.

  1. Station measures the train's stick: Two observers in the station frame drop arrow markers at the positions of each end of the moving stick simultaneously (by the station clocks). The gap between the markers gives the station's measurement.
  2. Train measures the station's stick: Two observers on the train drop markers at the positions of each end of the stationary stick simultaneously (by the train clocks). The gap gives the train's measurement.

Watch carefully — the two results are not the same. That discrepancy is the problem we fix in Steps 4 & 5.

Step 3

Violation of Einstein's Postulates

Comparing the two measurements from Step 2 reveals an asymmetry:

  • From the station: the train's metre stick measures the same as the station's.
  • From the train: the station's metre stick measures shorter than the train's.

This asymmetry means we can tell who is "really" moving — which directly violates Einstein's second postulate (the principle of relativity: the laws of physics are the same in all inertial frames).

Something must be wrong. We accepted the postulates at the start, so we must change our assumptions about lengths and times — not abandon the postulates.

Step 4

Invoking Length Contraction

At \(\beta = v/c = 0.6\), the Lorentz factor is \(\gamma = 1/\sqrt{1-\beta^2} = 1.25\).

We replace the train's metre stick with one that is contracted by a factor of \(1/\gamma = 0.8\) — that is, 4/5 of the station's metre stick.

This is length contraction: objects moving relative to an observer appear shortened along the direction of motion by the factor \(1/\gamma\).

Watch the metre stick on the train shrink to 4/5 of its former length. Now we are ready to repeat the measurements.

Step 5

Symmetry Restored

With the contracted metre stick on the train, we repeat all measurements:

  • The station measures the train's stick as 4/5 of the station metre. ✓
  • The train measures the station's stick as 4/5 of the train metre. ✓

Perfect symmetry — neither frame can claim to be "truly" at rest. Both see the other's metre stick as contracted by the same factor.

Time dilation follows too: measuring the speed of light using the contracted sticks now shows a discrepancy in time. We must slow the train's clocks to 4/5 of the station rate — after which both frames measure the same speed of light, and each sees the other's clocks running slow by the same factor.

Einstein's two postulates are satisfied. Length contraction and time dilation are not arbitrary — they are the necessary consequences of taking the postulates seriously.
